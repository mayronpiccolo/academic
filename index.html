About

Clinician, lecturer, and researcher — Cambridge, MA.

Born in Brazil, Dr. Mayron Piccolo is a Brazilian-American-Italian psychologist and Board Certified Behavior Analyst whose research, teaching, and clinical career are organized around a single question: how psychopathology can be understood, assessed, and treated more accurately across diverse populations. He pursues it through three connected arms.

Understanding asks how reward processes shape mental health — across eating and weight disorders (anorexia and bulimia nervosa, obesity, and binge eating), the altered reward circuitry of depression, and communal music-making as a social reward. Assessing asks whether our instruments carry the same meaning across languages and cultures, from the “several days” response option on the PHQ-9 to portion-size estimation errors and idioms of distress in trauma. Treating turns those findings into care and into interventions built to scale — and into direct clinical work with the communities that need them. Each arm includes digital implementation: telehealth psychotherapy with a multilingual immigrant caseload, fully remote experiments, and interventions designed to run without a clinician in the room.

He earned his PhD at the University of Fribourg (Switzerland), followed by research training at McLean Hospital and clinical training at Cambridge Health Alliance (both Harvard Medical School). A clinician as well as a scientist, he provides multilingual psychotherapy (English, Portuguese, Spanish, and French) to immigrant communities at Cambridge Health Alliance and consults to schools through McLean Hospital. He teaches award-winning courses on reward, psychopathology, and the psychology of music at Harvard, and has taught at Columbia, NYU, and universities in Switzerland and Brazil.

Current Projects

Treating Communal music Pilot paper forthcoming

First Tones

A reward-based intervention, built by applying a mechanism established experimentally: communal music acts as a social reward, repairing positive affect and strengthening belonging after social ostracism (Scientific Reports, 2025). A randomized controlled trial (communal music vs. communal drawing) now tests whether that mechanism helps preschoolers facing familial adversity feel and function better, and why — measuring mental and behavioral health, reward responses via child-friendly fNIRS imaging, and sense of belonging through a new observation tool. Behavioral findings from the pilot are coming out soon.

Assessing Eating & weight AN/BN paper forthcoming

Portion-Size Estimation in Eating Disorders

Portion perception is foundational to intake regulation: a portion must be perceived as large before it can trigger regulatory behavior. The Estimation Error Index (EEI) makes it measurable — a normalized index separating true perceptual distortion from scale-use artifacts. In anorexia and bulimia nervosa the distortion is magnitude-dependent, appearing specifically at large portions; the work now extends to binge-eating disorder, to culturally-relevant versions of the task, and into bariatric psychological assessment, where the EEI answers a gap in measuring eating psychopathology. A paper on the EEI in AN/BN is coming out soon.

Understanding Eating & weight Cross-cultural Analysis underway

Food Insecurity and Eating Disorders

Using the Healthy Minds Survey (n ≈ 1,876 South American students at U.S. universities, 2020–2025), this project asks how food insecurity — measured with the Hunger Vital Sign screener — relates to eating psychopathology and mental health in a population that is at once immigrant, young, and economically at risk.

Understanding Cross-cultural Paper forthcoming

Global Trauma Network

A 13-country collaboration (n ≈ 1,787) examining how child maltreatment and cultural social norms interact to shape mental health — depression, anxiety, trauma, resilience, and post-traumatic growth — across the US, Canada, Georgia, Germany, India, Iran, Israel, Macau, South Africa, Sweden, Switzerland, Türkiye, and the UK. A paper is coming out soon.

Assessing Cross-cultural Paper forthcoming

The “Several Days” Problem in Depression Screening (PHQ-9)

Using the U.S. Healthy Minds Survey, this measurement work shows that the shared response option “several days” on the PHQ-9 and GAD-7 screeners spans a wider severity range for students who speak a non-English language at home — semantic non-equivalence of a translated anchor. Replicated across survey waves and echoing his finding in the PHQ-9 Brazilian-Portuguese version. A paper is coming out soon.

Looking Ahead

Where each arm of the question goes next. Each includes digital implementation.

5 Years
  • Treating

    First Tones, Phase 2. Scale the communal music-making trial beyond the pilot, and build a digital, remotely delivered version of the communal-music intervention adapted for different age groups — from preschoolers through adults — developed with local mental health services for multilingual migrant communities.

  • Assessing

    Establish the Estimation Error Index (EEI) as a behavioral biomarker of eating pathology — a normalized measure that separates true perceptual distortion from scale-use artifacts. In anorexia and bulimia nervosa the distortion is magnitude-dependent, appearing specifically at large portions; the next step tests whether binge eating disorder shows a comparable or distinct profile.

  • Understanding

    Identify what drives large-portion distortion — loss of control over eating, negative urgency, and satiety responsiveness — and contrast it directly with the drive-for-thinness pathway seen in anorexia.

10 Years
  • Treating

    First Tones, Phases 3–4. Train lay facilitators and preschool teachers to deliver the intervention themselves, then test large-scale implementation — digital and in-person formats that reach people across ages and languages without depending on scarce clinical specialists.

  • Assessing

    EEI as a recalibratable treatment-response biomarker. Brief perceptual training that retunes portion estimation, and a measure sensitive enough to index response to behavioral and pharmacological treatment alike — including GLP-1 receptor agonists.

  • Understanding

    Mechanism-targeted, biomarker-guided treatment for eating disorders: precision care built on the perceptual and cognitive processes that maintain them, carried by a program that trains the next generation of clinical researchers.

Built in the Clinic

Assessing Eating & weight 2025 – Present

Bariatric Psychological Assessment

The pre-surgical psychological interview lacked measures of eating psychopathology. Redesigned the clinical interview guide with departmental leadership, adding validated and culturally relevant measures, and embedding research components that allow investigation of binge-eating mechanisms and their relation to post-surgical outcomes. The Estimation Error Index is one of the solutions developed for this gap.

Treating Cross-cultural 2025 – Present

Intercultural Training for Primary Care Clinicians

Proposed and led a training on culturally sensitive psychotherapy for PCBHI clinicians serving a predominantly immigrant caseload — addressing the gap between evidence-based protocols and the linguistic and cultural realities of the people receiving them. Builds on the finding that translated screening instruments can carry systematic measurement error (Brazilian Journal of Psychiatry, 2025).
